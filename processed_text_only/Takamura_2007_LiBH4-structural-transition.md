The electrical conductivity of lithium borohydride ( LiBH4 ) measured by the ac complex impedance method jumped by three orders of magnitude due to structural transition from orthorhombic to hexagonal at approximately 390 K. The hexagonal phase exhibited a high electrical conductivity of the order of 10 -3 S cm -1 . Furthermore, the conductivity calculated from the Nernst-Einstein equation using the correlation time obtained from 7 Li nuclear magnetic resonance was in good agreement with the measured electrical conductivity. It was concluded that the electrical conductivity in the hexagonal phase is due to the Li superionic conduction. ' 2007 American Institute of Physics . [ DOI: 10.1063/1.2817934 ]

Recently, we have reported that the microwave irradiation process can be applied to the dehydriding reaction of LiBH4. 1,2 The rate of the temperature increase in LiBH 4 by the microwave irradiation was drastically enhanced above 380 K, which resulted in a fast dehydriding reaction of LiBH4. At this temperature, LiBH4 undergoes a structural transition from orthorhombic [ low temperature ( LT ) phase ] to hexagonal [ high temperature ( HT ) phase ] . From the result of the permittivity measurements, it was suggested that LiBH4 is an insulator in the LT phase, while it might be conductive in the HT phase. 1 Therefore, probably due to the conductive loss, the HT phase was rapidly heated. Since hydrogen is covalently stabilized as [ BH4 ] -, which is then ionically combined with Li + , 3 it was predicted that the charge carrier is Li + and not H + .

Many investigations have been performed on LiBH 4 including those on the internal reorientation of [ BH4 ] -, 4,5 crystal structure, 6 high-pressure study, 7 and hydrogen storage properties. 8 However, there is no literature regarding studies on the motion of Li + in solid state LiBH4 ( typically below 550 K ) lytes in high-energy solid-state batteries, 9-20 it is fundamentally, and practically as well, significant to investigate the Li ionic conductivity of LiBH 4 .

Therefore, the purpose of the present study is to experimentally investigate both electrical conductivity and Li ionic motion in LiBH4. These were measured by the ac complex impedance method and 7 Li nuclear magnetic resonance ( NMR ) , respectively. The temperature dependence of the spin-lattice relaxation time T 1 was investigated by 7 Li NMR, which was compared with the electrical conductivities obtained from the ac complex impedance method in order to confirm whether the conduction carrier is Li + or not.

In summary, both the electrical conductivity and Li ionic motion in LiBH4 were experimentally investigated. The electrical conductivity measured by the ac complex impedance method jumped by three orders of magnitude due to structural transition at approximately 390 K. The HT phase exhibited a high electrical conductivity of the order of 10 -3 S cm -1 . The temperature dependence of the spin-lattice relaxation time T 1 showed a minimum at around 493 K, and it could be fitted by the BPP formula. The electrical conductivity calculated from the Nernst-Einstein equation using 7 Li NMR correlation times was in good agreement with that measured by the ac impedance method. From these results, it was concluded that the high electrical conductivity in the HT phase is a result of the Li superionic conduction.
